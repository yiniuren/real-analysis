Continuous Mappings

Topic: Limits and Continuity

Motivation

Continuity captures the idea that a function has "no surprises" — small changes in the input produce small changes in the output. This is the foundation for nearly everything in analysis: you cannot talk about limits, derivatives, or integrals in a meaningful way without first understanding when and how a function preserves closeness.

We also care about uniform continuity, a stronger cousin that says the function behaves equally well everywhere in its domain — no point is "harder" to control than any other. This distinction matters most on compact sets, where the two notions turn out to coincide.

Definitions

Definition 1 — Continuity

Let \((X, d_X)\) and \((Y, d_Y)\) be metric spaces. A mapping \(f: X \to Y\) is continuous if for every \(x \in X\) and every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(\delta > 0\) such that

$$d_X(x, y) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x), f(y)) < \varepsilon.$$

Intuition

Pick any point \(x\) and draw a tiny target circle of radius \(\varepsilon\) around \(f(x)\) in the output space. Continuity promises that you can always find a small enough input circle of radius \(\delta\) around \(x\) so that every point inside it lands inside that target. The catch: the size of \(\delta\) you need is allowed to depend on which \(x\) you picked. Near a steep part of the graph you might need a much smaller \(\delta\) than near a flat part.

Definition 2 — Uniform Continuity

A mapping \(f: X \to Y\) is uniformly continuous if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists \(\delta > 0\) such that for all \(x, y \in X\),

$$d_X(x, y) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x), f(y)) < \varepsilon.$$

In other words, the \(\delta\) does not depend on the choice of \(x\).

Intuition

Same picture as before, but now one single \(\delta\) must work for every point in the domain simultaneously. You can't let \(\delta\) shrink as you move around — it has to be a universal guarantee. For \(f(x) = x^2\) on all of \(\mathbb{R}\), this fails: far from the origin the graph is so steep that any fixed \(\delta\) eventually lets outputs escape the \(\varepsilon\)-band. But on a bounded interval like \([0, 3]\), the steepness is capped, so one \(\delta\) suffices everywhere.

Theorems

Theorem 1 — Equivalent Characterizations of Continuity

Let \(f: X \to Y\) be a mapping between metric spaces. The following are equivalent:

  1. \(f\) is continuous (Definition 1).
  2. The preimage of every open set in \(Y\) is open in \(X\).
  3. The preimage of every closed set in \(Y\) is closed in \(X\).
  4. For every sequence \((a_n) \subset X\) with \(a_n \to a\), we have \(f(a_n) \to f(a)\).
Proof sketch — (1) ⇒ (2)

Goal: show that if \(f\) satisfies the \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) definition, then \(f^{-1}(U)\) is open for every open \(U \subset Y\).

  • Let \(U \subset Y\) be open and pick any \(x \in f^{-1}(U)\).
    — We need to find a ball around \(x\) that stays inside \(f^{-1}(U)\).
  • Since \(f(x) \in U\) and \(U\) is open, there exists \(\varepsilon > 0\) with \(B_\varepsilon(f(x)) \subset U\).
    — Openness of \(U\) gives us a target radius in the output space.
  • By continuity (Definition 1) applied at \(x\) with this \(\varepsilon\), obtain \(\delta > 0\) such that \(d_X(x,y) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x),f(y)) < \varepsilon\).
    — This \(\delta\) converts the output guarantee into an input radius.
  • Then \(B_\delta(x) \subset f^{-1}(U)\), so \(f^{-1}(U)\) is open.
    — Every point of \(f^{-1}(U)\) is an interior point, which is exactly the definition of an open set.
Proof sketch — (2) ⇒ (1)

Goal: show that the open-preimage property implies the \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) condition at every point.

  • Fix \(x \in X\) and \(\varepsilon > 0\). Consider the open ball \(U = B_\varepsilon(f(x)) \subset Y\).
    — We construct an explicit open set whose preimage we can exploit.
  • By assumption, \(f^{-1}(U)\) is open in \(X\), and \(x \in f^{-1}(U)\).
    — Openness of the preimage guarantees a ball around \(x\) inside it.
  • So there exists \(\delta > 0\) with \(B_\delta(x) \subset f^{-1}(U)\), meaning \(d_X(x,y) < \delta \implies f(y) \in U \implies d_Y(f(x),f(y)) < \varepsilon\).
    — This is exactly the \(\varepsilon\)-\(\delta\) condition at \(x\).
Proof sketch — (1) ⇒ (4) (sequential continuity)

Goal: show that if \(a_n \to a\), then \(f(a_n) \to f(a)\).

  • Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\). By continuity at \(a\), obtain \(\delta > 0\) such that \(d_X(a, y) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(a), f(y)) < \varepsilon\).
    — We convert the continuity guarantee at \(a\) into a usable \(\delta\).
  • Since \(a_n \to a\), there exists \(N\) such that \(d_X(a_n, a) < \delta\) for all \(n \geq N\).
    — Convergence of the sequence lets us enter the \(\delta\)-ball eventually.
  • For all \(n \geq N\), \(d_Y(f(a_n), f(a)) < \varepsilon\). Since \(\varepsilon\) was arbitrary, \(f(a_n) \to f(a)\).
    — Combining the two bounds gives the sequential convergence we wanted.
Theorem 2 — Continuous Image of a Compact Set is Compact

Let \(f: X \to Y\) be continuous. If \(K \subset X\) is compact, then \(f(K) \subset Y\) is compact.

Proof

Goal: given an arbitrary open cover of \(f(K)\), extract a finite subcover.

  • Let \(\{V_\alpha\}\) be an open cover of \(f(K)\).
    — We start with any open cover of the image and need to reduce it to finitely many sets.
  • Since \(f\) is continuous, each \(f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\) is open in \(X\) (by Theorem 1, characterization (2)).
    — Pull the cover back to the domain, where we can use compactness of \(K\).
  • The collection \(\{f^{-1}(V_\alpha)\}\) covers \(K\), because every \(x \in K\) has \(f(x) \in V_\alpha\) for some \(\alpha\).
    — The preimages form an open cover of the compact set \(K\).
  • By compactness of \(K\), extract a finite subcover: \(K \subset f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_1}) \cup \cdots \cup f^{-1}(V_{\alpha_n})\).
    — This is the key step: compactness turns an infinite cover into a finite one.
  • Applying \(f\), we get \(f(K) \subset V_{\alpha_1} \cup \cdots \cup V_{\alpha_n}\).
    — Push the finite subcover forward to get a finite subcover of \(f(K)\). Done.
Corollary (of Theorem 2) — Extreme Value Theorem

Let \(f: X \to \mathbb{R}\) be continuous (where \(\mathbb{R}\) has the Euclidean metric) and let \(K \subset X\) be compact. Then \(f\) attains a maximum and a minimum value on \(K\).

Proof

Goal: show \(f(K)\) contains its supremum and infimum.

  • By Theorem 2, \(f(K)\) is a compact subset of \(\mathbb{R}\).
    — Continuity preserves compactness, so the image is compact in \(\mathbb{R}\).
  • A compact subset of \(\mathbb{R}\) is closed and bounded (Heine–Borel).
    — Boundedness guarantees that \(\sup f(K)\) and \(\inf f(K)\) are finite real numbers.
  • Since \(f(K)\) is closed, it contains all its limit points — in particular \(\sup f(K) \in f(K)\) and \(\inf f(K) \in f(K)\).
    — Closedness forces the extreme values to actually be achieved, not just approached.
Theorem 3 — Continuous on Compact Implies Uniformly Continuous

Let \(f: X \to Y\) be continuous and let \(K \subset X\) be compact. Then \(f\) is uniformly continuous on \(K\).

Proof

Goal: find a single \(\delta\) that works for all points in \(K\) simultaneously.

  • Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\). For each \(x \in K\), continuity gives \(\delta_x > 0\) with \(d_X(x, y) < \delta_x \implies d_Y(f(x), f(y)) < \varepsilon / 2\).
    — Each point has its own local \(\delta_x\); we need to unify them.
  • The open balls \(\{B_{\delta_x / 2}(x)\}_{x \in K}\) form an open cover of \(K\).
    — We use half-radius balls so the triangle inequality will work later.
  • By compactness, extract a finite subcover: \(K \subset B_{\delta_{x_1}/2}(x_1) \cup \cdots \cup B_{\delta_{x_n}/2}(x_n)\).
    — Compactness reduces infinitely many local guarantees to finitely many.
  • Set \(\delta = \frac{1}{2}\min(\delta_{x_1}, \ldots, \delta_{x_n}) > 0\).
    — The minimum of finitely many positive numbers is still positive — this is where finiteness is essential.
  • Now take any \(a, b \in K\) with \(d_X(a, b) < \delta\). Point \(a\) lies in some \(B_{\delta_{x_i}/2}(x_i)\), so \(d_X(a, x_i) < \delta_{x_i}/2\). Then \(d_X(b, x_i) \leq d_X(b, a) + d_X(a, x_i) < \delta + \delta_{x_i}/2 \leq \delta_{x_i}\).
    — Triangle inequality lets us route both \(a\) and \(b\) through the same center \(x_i\).
  • Therefore \(d_Y(f(a), f(b)) \leq d_Y(f(a), f(x_i)) + d_Y(f(x_i), f(b)) < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon\).
    — Both \(a\) and \(b\) are within \(\delta_{x_i}\) of \(x_i\), so both outputs are within \(\varepsilon/2\) of \(f(x_i)\). Adding gives \(\varepsilon\).

Examples

Example 1 — The identity is continuous

Let \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) be defined by \(f(x) = x\), where both the domain and image carry the Euclidean metric. Then \(f\) is continuous: for any \(x\) and any \(\varepsilon > 0\), choose \(\delta = \varepsilon\). Then \(|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| = |x - y| < \varepsilon\).

Example 2 — Continuity depends on the metric

Let \(f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}\) again be \(f(x) = x\), but now give the domain the Euclidean metric and the image the trivial (discrete) metric where \(d(a, b) = 0\) if \(a = b\) and \(d(a, b) = 1\) otherwise.

Then \(f\) is not continuous. Consider the open set \(U = [0, 1) \subset \mathbb{R}\) (which is open in the trivial metric because every subset is open in the discrete topology). Its preimage \(f^{-1}([0,1)) = [0, 1)\) is not open in the Euclidean metric on the domain, since no open ball around \(0\) is contained in \([0,1)\) restricted from the left.

This illustrates the worksheet's key note: continuity depends on the metric.

Exercises

Exercise 1

Prove that \(f(x) = 3x + 1\) is uniformly continuous on all of \(\mathbb{R}\) (with the Euclidean metric).

Show solution

Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), choose \(\delta = \varepsilon / 3\). Then \(|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| = 3|x - y| < 3 \cdot \varepsilon/3 = \varepsilon\). Since \(\delta\) does not depend on \(x\), the function is uniformly continuous.

Exercise 2

Show that \(f(x) = 1/x\) is continuous but not uniformly continuous on \((0, 1)\).

Show solution

Continuous: Fix \(x_0 \in (0,1)\) and \(\varepsilon > 0\). We have \(|1/x - 1/x_0| = |x - x_0|/(x \cdot x_0)\). For \(|x - x_0| < x_0/2\), we get \(x > x_0/2\), so \(|1/x - 1/x_0| < 2|x - x_0|/x_0^2\). Choosing \(\delta = \min(x_0/2,\; \varepsilon x_0^2/2)\) works.

Not uniformly continuous: Take \(x_n = 1/n\) and \(y_n = 1/(n+1)\). Then \(|x_n - y_n| = 1/(n(n+1)) \to 0\), but \(|f(x_n) - f(y_n)| = |n - (n+1)| = 1\). No single \(\delta\) can keep the outputs within \(\varepsilon = 1/2\).
