Convergence of Functions

Topic: Sequences and Series of Functions Prerequisites: Continuous Mappings

Motivation

When we have a sequence of functions \(f_1, f_2, f_3, \ldots\), we naturally ask: does this sequence "approach" some limit function \(f\)? The answer depends on how we measure closeness. Pointwise convergence checks each input \(x\) separately — as long as the numbers \(f_n(x)\) converge to \(f(x)\), we're happy. But this is a weak guarantee: different points can converge at completely different speeds, and nice properties like continuity can be lost in the limit.

Uniform convergence is the stronger notion. It demands that the entire graph of \(f_n\) settles into an \(\varepsilon\)-tube around \(f\) at the same rate everywhere. This uniformity is what lets us swap limits with integrals and preserve continuity — the results that make analysis actually work.

Definitions

Definition 1 — Pointwise Convergence

Let \((f_n)_{n=1}^{\infty}\) be a sequence of functions defined on a set \(A \subset \mathbb{R}\). We say \((f_n)\) converges pointwise to \(f : A \to \mathbb{R}\) on \(A\) if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) and every \(x \in A\), there exists \(N = N(\varepsilon, x) \in \mathbb{N}\) such that for all \(n \geq N\),

$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon.$$

Intuition

Fix any single point \(x\). The sequence of numbers \(f_1(x), f_2(x), f_3(x), \ldots\) converges to \(f(x)\). That's it — pointwise convergence is just ordinary convergence of real numbers, repeated separately for each \(x\). The crucial detail is that the "how long you have to wait" number \(N\) is allowed to depend on which \(x\) you're looking at. Near a trouble spot, \(N\) might be enormous; at a calm point, \(N\) might be small.

Definition 2 — Uniform Convergence

The sequence \((f_n)\) converges uniformly to \(f : A \to \mathbb{R}\) on \(A\) if for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(N = N(\varepsilon) \in \mathbb{N}\) such that for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x \in A\),

$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon.$$

The key difference from pointwise convergence: \(N\) depends only on \(\varepsilon\), not on \(x\).

Intuition

Draw the graph of \(f\) and inflate it into a tube of height \(2\varepsilon\). Uniform convergence means that from some index \(N\) onward, the entire graph of \(f_n\) fits inside that tube — no part of the curve pokes out anywhere. One single \(N\) tames every point simultaneously. Think of it as the "speed of convergence" being bounded across the whole domain: no point is allowed to converge arbitrarily slowly.

Theorems

Proposition 1 — Sup-Norm Characterization

\((f_n)\) converges to \(f\) uniformly on \(A\) if and only if

$$\sup_{x \in A} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty.$$

Proof

Goal: show the sup-norm condition is equivalent to the definition of uniform convergence.

  • (⇒) Suppose \(f_n \to f\) uniformly. Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(N\) such that \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon\) for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x \in A\).
    — Taking the supremum over all \(x\), we get \(\sup_{x \in A} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \varepsilon\) for \(n \geq N\).
  • (⇐) Suppose \(\sup_{x \in A} |f_n(x) - f(x)| \to 0\). Given \(\varepsilon > 0\), choose \(N\) so that \(\sup_{x} |f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon\) for all \(n \geq N\).
    — Since \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \sup_{x}|f_n - f|\) for every \(x\), we get \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon\) for all \(x \in A\) and all \(n \geq N\). This is exactly uniform convergence.
Theorem 2 — Uniform Limit of Continuous Functions is Continuous

Assume \((f_n)\) converges to \(f\) uniformly on \(A\), and each \(f_n\) is continuous at \(x_0 \in A\). Then \(f\) is also continuous at \(x_0\).

Proof

Goal: show \(|f(x) - f(x_0)| < \varepsilon\) for \(x\) near \(x_0\), using the triangle inequality to route through \(f_n\).

  • Let \(\varepsilon > 0\). By uniform convergence, choose \(N\) so that \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon / 3\) for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x \in A\).
    — This pins \(f_N\) within \(\varepsilon/3\) of \(f\) everywhere.
  • Since \(f_N\) is continuous at \(x_0\), choose \(\delta > 0\) so that \(|x - x_0| < \delta \implies |f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| < \varepsilon / 3\).
    — Continuity of the single function \(f_N\) gives us control in the middle.
  • For \(|x - x_0| < \delta\), apply the triangle inequality: $$|f(x) - f(x_0)| \leq \underbrace{|f(x) - f_N(x)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} + \underbrace{|f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} + \underbrace{|f_N(x_0) - f(x_0)|}_{< \varepsilon/3} < \varepsilon.$$
    — The three-part split: uniform convergence handles the first and third terms, continuity of \(f_N\) handles the middle.
Corollary 3

Let \(f_n \in C[0, 1]\) (continuous on \([0, 1]\)) converge to \(f\) pointwise on \([0, 1]\). If the convergence is in fact uniform, then \(f \in C[0, 1]\).

Contrapositive: if the pointwise limit \(f\) is discontinuous, then the convergence is not uniform. (This is why \(x^n \to f\) on \([0, 1]\) cannot be uniform — the limit is discontinuous at \(x = 1\).)

Theorem 4 — Interchange of Limit and Integral

Assume \(f_n\) are Riemann integrable on \([0, 1]\) and \(f_n \to f\) uniformly. Then

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_0^1 f_n(x)\, dx = \int_0^1 f(x)\, dx.$$

In other words, uniform convergence lets us swap the limit and the integral.

Proof sketch

Goal: bound \(\left|\int_0^1 f_n - \int_0^1 f\right|\) using the sup-norm.

  • We have \(\left|\int_0^1 f_n(x)\,dx - \int_0^1 f(x)\,dx\right| = \left|\int_0^1 (f_n(x) - f(x))\,dx\right| \leq \int_0^1 |f_n(x) - f(x)|\,dx\).
    — Linearity of the integral and the triangle inequality.
  • Since \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| \leq \sup_{x} |f_n - f|\) for all \(x\), we get \(\int_0^1 |f_n - f|\,dx \leq \sup_x |f_n(x) - f(x)| \cdot 1\).
    — Replace the integrand by its uniform bound and integrate over \([0, 1]\).
  • By Proposition 1, \(\sup |f_n - f| \to 0\), so the integral difference tends to 0.
    — The sup-norm characterization closes the argument.
Important Warnings

(1) Pointwise convergence does not imply that integrals converge. The pointwise limit can redistribute mass in ways that change the integral.

(2) Even uniform convergence does not guarantee that derivatives converge: \(f_n \to f\) uniformly does not imply \(f_n' \to f'\). Differentiation is a much more delicate operation than integration.

Examples

Example 1 — Uniform convergence: \(f_n(x) = x + 1/n\)

On all of \(\mathbb{R}\), \(f_n(x) = x + 1/n\) converges to \(f(x) = x\). The sup-norm is \(\sup_x |f_n(x) - f(x)| = \sup_x |1/n| = 1/n \to 0\). Since the supremum does not depend on \(x\) at all, convergence is uniform.

Example 2 — Pointwise but not uniform: \(f_n(x) = x + x^2/n\)

On \(\mathbb{R}\), \(f_n(x) = x + x^2/n\) converges to \(f(x) = x\) pointwise: for each fixed \(x\), \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| = x^2/n \to 0\). But \(\sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} x^2/n = \infty\) for every \(n\), so the convergence is not uniform. At large \(x\), the error \(x^2/n\) can be made arbitrarily large.

Example 3 — The classic: \(f_n(x) = x^n\) on \([0, 1]\)

For \(x \in [0, 1)\), \(x^n \to 0\). At \(x = 1\), \(1^n = 1\) for all \(n\). So the pointwise limit is

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0, & x \in [0, 1) \\ 1, & x = 1. \end{cases}$$

This limit is discontinuous at \(x = 1\). Since each \(f_n(x) = x^n\) is continuous and the limit is not, Theorem 2 (contrapositive) tells us the convergence is not uniform on \([0, 1]\).

However, on the restricted domain \([0, 0.9]\), \(\sup_{x \in [0, 0.9]} x^n = 0.9^n \to 0\), so the convergence is uniform on \([0, 0.9]\). The trouble is isolated near \(x = 1\).

Exercises

Exercise 1

Prove that if \(f_n \to f\) uniformly on \(A\), then \(f_n \to f\) pointwise on \(A\). Give an example showing the converse fails.

Show solution

Uniform convergence means: for every \(\varepsilon > 0\), there exists \(N\) such that \(|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon\) for all \(n \geq N\) and all \(x\). In particular, for any fixed \(x\), this \(N\) works, so \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\). The converse fails: \(f_n(x) = x^n\) on \([0, 1]\) converges pointwise but not uniformly (Example 3).

Exercise 2

Let \(f_n(x) = \frac{nx}{1 + nx}\) on \([0, 1]\). Find the pointwise limit. Is the convergence uniform?

Show solution

For \(x = 0\): \(f_n(0) = 0\) for all \(n\), so \(f(0) = 0\). For \(x > 0\): \(f_n(x) = \frac{nx}{1 + nx} = \frac{1}{1/(nx) + 1} \to 1\) as \(n \to \infty\). So \(f(x) = 0\) if \(x = 0\), and \(f(x) = 1\) if \(x > 0\). This limit is discontinuous at 0, but each \(f_n\) is continuous. By the contrapositive of Theorem 2, the convergence is not uniform on \([0, 1]\).

Exercise 3

Show that \(f_n(x) = x/n\) converges uniformly to \(0\) on \([0, 1]\) but not on \([0, \infty)\).

Show solution

On \([0, 1]\): \(\sup_{x \in [0,1]} |x/n| = 1/n \to 0\). Uniform by Proposition 1. On \([0, \infty)\): \(\sup_{x \geq 0} |x/n| = \infty\) for every \(n\). The sup-norm never tends to 0, so convergence is not uniform.
